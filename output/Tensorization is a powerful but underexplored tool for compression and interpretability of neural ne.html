Abstract

Tensorizing a neural network involves reshaping some or all of its dense weight matrices into higher-order tensors and approximating them using low-rank tensor network decompositions. This technique has shown promise as a model compression strategy for large-scale neural networks. However, despite encouraging empirical results, tensorized neural networks (TNNs) remain underutilized in mainstream deep learning. In this position paper, we offer a perspective on both the potential and current limitations of TNNs. We argue that TNNs represent a powerful yet underexplored framework for deep learningâ€”one that deserves greater attention from both engineering and theoretical communities. Beyond compression, we highlight the value of TNNs as a flexible class of architectures with distinctive scaling properties and increased interpretability. A central feature of TNNs is the presence of bond indices, which introduce new latent spaces not found in conventional networks. These internal representations may provide deeper insight into the evolution of features across layers, potentially advancing the goals of mechanistic interpretability. We conclude by outlining several key research directions aimed at overcoming the practical barriers to scaling and adopting TNNs in modern deep learning workflows.

1 Introduction

If the scaling laws of neural networks are to be believed, we will need to keep increasing their size â€” and cost â€” in order to produce more intelligence Kaplan . Yet, neural networks clearly cannot continue to grow indefinitely. The rapid expansion in their size brings two immediate challenges. First, there is the task of effectively compressing massive dense models that have already been developed â€” and doing so without significantly degrading performance Xunyu . This issue goes beyond mere practical or business concerns. Of course, compression enables the deployment of large models on resource-constrained hardware (e.g., edge devices in robots or autonomous vehicles), thereby increasing their business value and accessibility. But more fundamentally, the development of high-quality compression techniques is already a step toward discovering more efficient, compact parameterizations of generic neural networks: What are the most relevant degrees of freedom that actually carry information â€“ or high-level knowledge â€“ within a dense neural network?

Large neural networks are often significantly overparameterized. In large language models, for example, only a few layers â€“ or even just a small number of individual neurons â€“ are frequently found to be highly correlated with the modelâ€™s output Maini ; Mengxia ; Ghorbani . Many of the remaining layers can often be pruned with little to no loss in performance DistilBERT . While maintaining a large model during training and applying compression afterward may be an effective training strategy â€“ perhaps due to current limitations in optimization methods â€“ this work focuses on reparameterizing models through low-rank factorizations. Beyond traditional pruning approaches, the dense weight matrices in neural networks may often admit low-rank tensor network (TN) decompositions along directions that are not always intuitive. This observation opens the door to replacing some or all dense layers with structured TN layers. The resulting architectures, which mix dense and tensorized components, are known as tensorized neural networks (TNNs). When effective, these tensor network decompositions not only reduce the number of parameters, but also reveal fine-grained correlation structures within the weight matrices. In such cases, the geometry of the TN reflects these underlying correlations.111 Tensor networks were originally developed in quantum physics to efficiently represent the wavefunctions of large quantum many-body systems White . For example, the wavefunction of a quantum magnet or a superfluid on a lattice is a high-dimensional vector whose size grows exponentially with the number of lattice sites, making direct computations intractable. However, such wavefunctions at low energies typically exhibit limited, structured correlations, which can be exploited using tensor networks. These networks compactly represent the wavefunction using far fewer parametersâ€”often scaling only linearly with system sizeâ€”by encoding only the relevant correlations b1 ; cirac . The TN structure of a model may either emerge naturally from the pretrained weights â€“ potentially differing across layers and models â€“ or be hardwired into the model as an inductive bias during training. In the latter case, a well-chosen TN architecture could improve both training efficiency and model accuracy. A key motivation for expecting compressibility is that real-world data is often highly structured and correlated Bermudez-Edo ; Graff . It is therefore reasonable to expect that the weights and activations of neural networks trained on such data would inherit similar structure â€“ and thus be amenable to compression via tensor network techniques.

In this position paper, we first argue, in Sec.Â 3, why TNNs warrant greater attention and research effort â€“ both as a compression technique and as a compelling subclass of deep neural networks in their own right. TNNs have primarily been explored as a tool for model compression, as illustrated in Fig.Â 1. In this approach, all or part of the dense weight matrices in a pretrained network are first reshaped into higher-order tensors, which are then decomposed into low-rank tensor networks. As a compression technique, tensorization is complementary to more traditional methods such as quantization Xiao ; Liu , pruning Sun ; Ma_prune , and knowledge distillation Hinton . Crucially, tensorization can be combined with these other techniques to achieve higher overall compression. For instance, one can apply tensorization to an already quantized or pruned model, or conversely, quantize or prune a tensorized model. Similarly, a dense teacher model can be distilled into a tensorized student model. In many cases, combining these strategies outperforms applying them in isolation Razvan ; Ribeiro ; Nianyin .

Beyond compressing pretrained models, TNNs have also been trained from scratch for specific tasks. In this setting, TNNs represent an interesting subclass of neural networks: while the overall information flow between layers remains sequential, the internal tensor network structure of each layer can give rise to complex, non-sequential computation patterns within the layer. This internal structure introduces a significantly richer hyperparameter space compared to standard dense layers, offering greater flexibility in architectural design. We also explore how TNNs introduce a distinctive inductive bias into neural networks â€” one that remains poorly understood but may offer useful properties. We propose a new perspective, called the â€œstack viewâ€ of TNNs, which interprets this bias as a form of structured sparsity in the weights. In addition, we discuss how TNNs offer inherent opportunities for accelerating both forward and backward passes during training and inference. Finally, we highlight their potential for advancing mechanistic interpretability. Then in Sec.Â 4, we explain why TNNs, despite their advantages, have not seen widespread adoption. We identify key challenges limiting their development and propose research directions to address them. We also outline a broader vision for building what we call fully tensorized neural networksâ€”models where all components, including weights, data, activations, activation functions, and normalization layers, are represented using tensor networks. We believe such architectures could enable the next generation of scalable and efficient neural networks.

3 Why Tensorized Neural Networks are awesome

In this section, we argue why tensorized neural networks are an interesting class of neural networks that deserve further attention and research development. Then, in Sec.Â 4, we describe the current limitations and challenges to building state-of-the-art models with TNNs.

Tensorization injects a relevant inductive bias into neural networks.â€“ An inductive bias sets a hypothesis about training data that can be generalized to unseen data Utgoff ; Goyal . A general framework for understanding inductive biases is provided by geometric deep learning Jonathan , which, for instance, connects many inductive biases to enforcing symmetries on the weights. For instance, the success of convolutional neural networks can be traced to the fact that their layers are designed to respect translation equivariance Misiakiewicz . The inductive bias introduced by TNNs appears to have a different origin, not one rooted in obvious symmetries. Instead, it can be understood as resulting from constraint on the â€œspatialâ€ correlations between the weights.333As mentioned previously, weights inside a tensorized layer are spatially arranged in a high-dimensional hypercube (this corresponds to reshaping the weight matrix as a higher-order tensor). The bond dimensions of a TN then inform the correlations between different directions of the cube. In general, a higher bond dimension allows for more complex correlations between weights, while a smaller bond dimension restricts them. Bond dimension equal to one, Fig.Â 3(iii), corresponds to the absence of correlations. But why should we expect the weights to be correlated in this way? As previously mentioned, real-world data is often highly structured and exhibits strong correlations. It is therefore reasonable to expect that the learned weights of a neural network also reflect this structure â€“ suggesting that relatively small bond dimensions in the corresponding tensor network may suffice. This idea also aligns with the view that gradient descent tends to align the statistical structure of the data, weights, and gradients throughout training (see, e.g., Radhakrishnan ). The main challenge for tensorization is then to identify tensor networks that best reflect the correlation structure of the weights in a given layer.

TNNs have a richer hyperparameter space and can be scaled in several directions.â€“ A standard dense fully-connected layer can be scaled by simply enlarging the dimensions (number of hidden neurons) of the weight matrix.
